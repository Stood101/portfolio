Introduction¶

Project Overview We applied unsupervised learning to a social‑media engagement dataset—likes, shares, comments and reaction emojis—without any predefined labels.

Methodology¶

Data Preparation: Cleaned, imputed and scaled all metrics.

Dimensionality Reduction: Used PCA to extract the most informative features.

Clustering: Employed K‑means to group posts with similar engagement patterns.

Interpretation: Profiled each cluster to see which content types drive the most “loves,” comments and shares.

Insights from Scree Plot¶

Initial Sharp Drop: The plot shows a sharp drop in the explained variance ratio from the first to the second principal component, where the first component explains 40.7% of the variance in the data. This suggests that the first principal component is very important and captures most of the data's variance.

Gradual Decline: After the first component, the explained variance decreases gradually, indicating that the subsequent components contribute less to explaining the variance in the data. This is common in many datasets, where a small number of components capture the majority of the variance.

Choosing Components: The vertical red line at Component 3 suggests that 3 principal components explain the majority of the variance. Based on the plot, it's clear that after the first three components, the additional components have diminishing contributions to the explained variance.

Elbow Criterion: This plot is consistent with the elbow method for selecting the number of principal components. The "elbow" in the plot occurs at Component 3, where the explained variance ratio starts to level off.

Variance Ratios: The explained variance ratios are printed below the plot, with the first component explaining 40.7% of the variance, the second explaining 12.9%, and the third explaining 11.6%. The remaining components each explain progressively smaller portions of the variance.

Interpretation¶

Cluster Formation:

The dendrogram illustrates how data points are grouped into clusters based on Ward's method, with the distance representing dissimilarity between clusters. Larger distances show that clusters are more distinct from each other.

Cluster Merging:

At the start, individual data points or small clusters are separated. As we move up the plot, these smaller clusters start merging into larger ones, showing how clusters with similar characteristics combine.

Color-Coded Clusters:

The color-coded branches (green, red, purple) highlight different clusters. Green and red clusters merge first, indicating they are more similar, while the purple clusters merge later, showing they are less similar to the others.

Optimal Clustering Point:

The large gap between clusters at distance ~30 suggests a point where the tree can be "cut" to define the ideal number of clusters. This gap is key in deciding the best number of clusters to form, as large gaps represent clusters that are very distinct from one another.

Key Insight:

Ward’s method ensures that the clusters formed are as compact as possible, reducing internal variance. The point at which the dendrogram is cut helps to determine how many clusters make sense for the analysis, focusing on similarity within each cluster.

Best Model Selection and Rationale:¶

Best Model: Model 1 (Original x-features)¶

Rationale:

Higher AUC: Model 1 has the highest AUC score (0.79), indicating that it is the most effective model for distinguishing between video and non-video posts.

Lower False Negatives: The confusion matrix for Model 1 has fewer false negatives (118)**, indicating fewer video posts are missed compared to Models 2 and 3.

Interpretability: The original features (such as likes, shares, and comments) likely provide more intuitive and meaningful insights into the content type, whereas PCA and clustering reduce the data and may miss subtle patterns.

Consistency: Model 1 provides the most consistent results across both training and testing datasets, with a minimal train-test gap (0.009), suggesting that the model generalizes well.

Final Selection: Model 1 (Original x-features) is the best choice for predicting whether a post is a video or not due to its higher AUC, accuracy, and better handling of false negatives compared to other models.

Confusion Matrix Interpretation:¶

In Model 1 (Original x-features), the model correctly predicted 98 video posts (True Positives), while 8 non-video posts were incorrectly classified as videos (False Positives). There were 118 video posts mistakenly classified as non-videos (False Negatives), which is a key error since it could lead to missed opportunities for video promotion. The model also correctly predicted 485 non-video posts (True Negatives). The primary risk here is the false negatives, as missing video content can lead to decreased engagement with video posts.

Final Model Selection:¶

Model 1 (Original x-features) is selected as the best model due to its highest AUC score (0.79) and accuracy (82.2%), which indicates strong performance in distinguishing between video and non-video posts. It also has the lowest false negatives (118), meaning fewer video posts are missed compared to Models 2 and 3. The model uses intuitive features like likes, comments, and shares, which make it easier to understand and interpret. Additionally, the small train-test gap (0.009) suggests the model generalizes well to unseen data, providing a good balance between performance and simplicity.

Conclusion:¶

Model 1, using the original x-features, is the best model for predicting whether a post is a video or not. It has the highest AUC, the lowest false negatives, and the best balance between train-test performance. While PCA and K-means models are useful for dimensionality reduction and clustering, respectively, they do not outperform the original feature-based approach in this case.
